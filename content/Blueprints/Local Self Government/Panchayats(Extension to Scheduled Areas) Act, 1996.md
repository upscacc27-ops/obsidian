
### Introduction
Tribal communities in India make about 8.6% of the population.
Areas with significant tribal population are designated Scheduled Areas by the President under [[Art 244 Administration of Scheduled Areas & Tribal Areas]] so that tribals can have control over their local resources, social customs & development. 
73rd amendment did not apply to Scheduled areas automatically
1996 : the Panchayats(extension to Scheduled areas) was passed, giving similar self-governing power to tribal communities in scheduled areas.


### Objectives
- extend provisions of Part IX of the Constitution to 5th schedule areas
- provide self-rule to tribal population in scheduled areas, governance through traditional systems
- establish tribal gram sabhas as centres of governance
- evolve an administrative framework consistent with traditional tribal practices
- safeguard customs, traditions, tribal identity
- prevent PRIs at higher levels from subsuming power of gram sabhas
- grant tribal communities rights over local natural resources

### Key provisions
- extends Panchayati Raj provisions to 5th schedule areas along with necessary modifications
- gram sabha as nucleus/centre of governance & participatory democracy in these areas
- gram sabha could approve plans, projects before implementation
- consultation with Gram sabha is required before land acquisition & rehab
- tribal communities ownership over minor forest produce, minor water bodies, minor minerals & village markets
- authority to prevent & restore unlawful transfer of tribal land
- minimum 50% seats to be reserved for STs, chairperson posts reserved for STs


### Significance of PESA
- Strengthens democratic decentralization -makes gram sabha core decision making body in scheduled areas
- ensures tribal self rule - legal recognition to traditional tribal governance & enables direct participation, include women
- protects land rights & prevents land alienation - mandatory consent
- Grants ownership & control over natural resources (jal, jangal, zameen) 
- promotes community led economic development - reduced dependence on external factors
- promotes sustainable resource management
- safeguards cultural identity & customs - legal recognition to customs, dispute resolution systems
- improves welfare delivery & accountability

### Challenges
- Partial & uneven implementation - Jharkhand notified PESA rules 30 years later, Rajasthan took long to operationalize regulations
- Conflict with other laws - forest & mining laws creates confusion
- Followed in letter, not in spirit - projects approved without genuine Gram Sabha deliberation
- Weak devolution of powers & fiscal autonomy
- Administrative hurdles & bureaucratic resistance, lack of political will
- political interference - especially in resource rich states like Odisha, Chhattisgarh
- States define gram sabha powers differently, leading to inconsistency, weakens uniform implementation
- Limited coverage of tribal population - applies only to 5th schedule areas

### Way forward
- time-bound implementation - frame & implement PESA rules to all 5th schedule areas & amend conflicting laws
- Binding gram sabha consent - redefine consultation as free, prior & informed consent
- legal convergence - Align PESA with Forest Rights Act
- Fiscal empowerment - ensure direct devolution
- monitoring & grievance redressal mechanisms - institutionalize social audits, digital tracking, setup dedicated grievance redressal mechanisms
- capacity & awareness - training


### Conclusion
PESA act holds transformative potential for strengthening tribal local self governance in 5th schedule areas. 
empowers Gram sabha, protects land & forest rights, recognizes traditional institutions
However, its success depends upon sincere implementation, legal convergence and administrative commitment.
effective execution can ensure protection of *Jal, Jungle, Jameen* while promoting inclusive & sustainable development for tribal communities
