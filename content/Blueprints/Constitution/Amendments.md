### Procedural Limitations
- Requirement of special majority for amendments under Art 368.
- ratification of at least half of the states to amend federal provisions like elections of the President, distribution of powers, SC & HCs, Rajya Sabha
- Presidential assent required 
- Judicial Review - Constitutional amendments are subject to JR, including on procedural grounds as affirmed by SC in [[1973 Keshavananda Bharti v UOI]]

### Substantive Limitations
- Basic Structure Doctrine - [[1973 Keshavananda Bharti v UOI]] - Parliament can amend any part but cannot alter or destroy the basic structure, later affirmed in [[1975 Indira Nehru Gandhi v Raj Narain]], [[1980 Minerva Mills v Union of India]], [[2007 IR Coelho v State of Tamil Nadu]]
- Limited amending power is part of the Basic Structure - as held in [[1980 Minerva Mills v Union of India]]

### Need for amendments
- ensures social justice, social equality, inclusive development - 1st CAA abolished Zamindari System, enabled land reforms - equitable distribution
- affirmative action - 1st, 93rd, 103rd CAAs expanded reservations for SC, ST & EWS, institutionalized protection under 65th,69th CAA -establishment of relevant bodies
- decentralization - local participation - 73rd & 74th CAA
- Cooperative federalism - 101st CAA - GST Reforms
- Political accountability - Anti Defection Law (52nd CAA 1985), Limitation on size of COM 91st CAA 2003.
	- Flexibility in governance- 69th CAA - special status to NCT of Delhi
- Gender justice - 106th CAA 2023 - Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam - 1/3rd reservation in Lok Sabha & State legislatures, 73rd & 74th CAA - 1/3rd res in local governance bodies, 42nd CAA - Fundamental duty to avoid practices that undermine women's diginity

### Criticism of the procedure
- Centralized process - only Parliament has the power to initiate
- Weak role of states - only some amendments require ratification, a US constitutional amendment requires 2/3rds vote in both the houses, then ratification by 3/4th of states
- No time limit for state ratification
- No provision for joint sitting in deadlocks
- Frequent Amendments - amended over 100 times, US amended only 27 times in a longer time of existence
- Vagueness of Art 368 - needs judicial interpretation
- No special body like Australia, Ireland & Switzerland

### Conclusion
India's amendment process maintains its relevance in an evolving society. 
However, it encounters significant obstacles, including political disagreements, judicial interventions & procedural complexities.
Tackling these issues requires a balanced strategy that uphold the fundamental ideals while ensuring flexibility & adaptability
