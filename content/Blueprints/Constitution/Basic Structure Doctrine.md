### Limitations places by this doctrine on the amenability 
- parliament can amend any part of the constitution however cannot destroy the basic structure 
- limited amending power itself is the basic structure - [[1980 Minerva Mills v Union of India]]
- supremacy of the constitution indestructible -- [[1975 Indira Nehru Gandhi v Raj Narain]]
- constitution amendments cannot be immunized from scrutiny - [[2007 IR Coelho v State of Tamil Nadu]]
- constitutional amendments cannot destroy federalism - [[1994 S. R. Bommai v UOI]]

### Significance
- prevents authoritarianism - constitutional safeguard against majoritarianism 
- maintains constitutional supremacy - as reiterated in [[1980 Minerva Mills v Union of India]]
- Protects Core constitutional values 
- ensures balance between flexibility & rigidity
- strengthens judicial review - [[2007 IR Coelho v State of Tamil Nadu]]
- protects FRs - cannot destroy the essential content
- Stabilizes democracy - especially during emergency

### Critique
- Counter-majoritarian & Undemocratic - criticized for providing judicial supremacy over parliamentary sovereignty
- no explicitly mentioned - judicial creation
- vagueness & uncertainty - lacks precise definition & continuous expansion
- Obstructs progressive reforms - invalidation of NJAC

### Reforms
- Clarification & codification of doctrine - scope
- Constitutional Review Commission - comprising of judiciary, executive, civil society to provide another layer of accountability
- Tempering the application - apply only when direct & definite threat to core values

### Conclusion
Basic Structure Doctrine requires careful application & possible refinements. It remains indispensable to preservation of India's constitutional supremacy. 
As India develops through the challenges of modern governance, this doctrine will strike an appropriate balance between the power of government's branches, asserting its place in the heart of constitutional jurisprudence
