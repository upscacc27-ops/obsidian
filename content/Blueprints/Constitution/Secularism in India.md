### Introduction
Secularism is an ideology that advocates the separation of religion from state & ensures governmental neutrality in matters of religion

### Types
- Positive - 
	- no complete separation, state maintains a distance but can interfere to protect, reform & regulate
	- no discrimination based on religion
	- entails a presence of govt. in religious institutions (Ex - Central Waqf board & Temple Devasam boards), religion in govt institutions (Madrasas, Sanskrit Pathshala)
- Negative
	- complete separation, state does not interfere at all
	- no discrimination based on religion
	- follows complete neutrality

### Constitutional Provisions supporting secularism
- [[Art 25 Freedom of conscience & free profession, practice &   propagation of religion]]
- [[Art 26 Freedom to manage religious affairs]]
- [[Art 27 Freedom as to payments of taxes for promotion of religion]]
- [[Art 28 Freedom as to attendance of religious instruction in educational institutions]]
- [[Art 29 Protection of interests of minorities]] - Art 29 & 30 protects cultural & educational rights of minorities
- [[Art 30 Rights of minorities to establish & administer educational institutions]]
- [[Art 16 Equality of Opportunity in Public Employment]]  prohibits discrimination on grounds of religions etc.
- [[Art 14 Equality before Law & Equal Protection of Laws]] 
- [[Art 51A Fundamental duties]] promote harmony & spirit of common brotherhood
- [[Preamble]] - addition of term 'Secular'

### Supreme Court judgements
- [[1973 Keshavananda Bharti v UOI]] - secularism is a part of basic structure, cannot be amended or destroyed
- 1974 Ahmedabad St. Xavier College v State of Gujarat - SC held secularism does not mean anti-religious, but respect for all. Affirmed that MEIs have the right to manage affairs under [[Art 30 Rights of minorities to establish & administer educational institutions]]
- 1983 SP Mittal v UOI - government officials cannot participate in religious activities while on duty, public funds cannot be used for religious activities (unconstitutional)
- [[1994 S. R. Bommai v UOI]] - secularism does not mean atheism, but equal protection & treatment of all religions. govt. acting against secular principles can face constitutional consequences
- [[2018 Indian Young Lawyers Association v State of Kerala]] - religious practices are subject to constitutional principles.
- 2024 Balram Singh v UOI - Challenge to preamble - challenging inclusion of Socialist & Secular, dismissed

### Challenges
- Communalism - has been a use of religious identity to divide society, leading to violent conflicts. Ex - 1984 anti-sikh, 1992-93 mumbai riots, 2002 godhra riots & 2020 delhi anti-CAA
- Politicization - exploit religious sentiments to create vote banks, writ petition of 2022 before SC seeking ban on political parties that use names & symbols with religious connotations
- Majoritarianism - rise of ideology of India as a Hindu Nation, increasing insecurity among minorities - mob lynching, *love jihad*, forced reconversions *ghar vapsi*
- Radicalization - rise of Islamic fundamentalism, revivalism
- minority insecurity - rise of hate speech, discrimination
- weakening of Indian judiciary - judicial barbarism - reluctance to take on cases that challenge significant govt. policies

### Way forward
- Education as a transformative tool - encourage inter-faith dialogue & mutual respect. reduce prejudice through awareness & civic learning
- promote social reforms - Uniform civil code, gender justice & equality
- secular politics -issue focused development - stricter enforcement of electoral laws against hate speech
- combat religious extremism - firm measures against radical groups - culture of tolerance  & peaceful coexistence
- Protect minority rights

### Conclusion
Indian Secularism is a positive & accommodative model. However it faces political, social & ideological challenges. Strengthening constitutional values, inclusive development & civic responsibility is essential to preserve India's secular fabric
