- 50th anniversary of Bonded Labor System (Abolition) Act, 1976

### Introduction
system of forced labor, person compelled to work to repay a debt or advance taken by them or family, in most cases, debt is small but carries high interest, making repayment nearly impossible
laborer remains trapped in cycle of exploitation, often working for little to no wages

### Constitutional & Statutory provisions
- [[Art 23 Prohibition of forced labour]]
- [[Art 21 Right to life and personal liberty]]
- [[Art 39 DPSP Principles of policy to be followed by state]] - directs State to protect children & workers from exploitation
- [[Art 41 DPSP Aim to create a welfare state]] - assistance in case of unemployment
- [[Art 43 DPSP Living wage etc. for workers]] - secure living wages & decent working conditions
- Bonder Labor System(Abolition) Act, 1976 - principle legislation related to bonded labor, abolished it & extinguishes all related debts
- Child & Adolescent Labor (Prohibition & Regulation) Act - prohibits employment of children below 14 years & Restricts 14-18 year old from employment in hazardous conditions

### Judicial Backing
- 1984 Bandhua Mukti Morcha v UOI - SC held that bonded labor violates Art 21 & Art 23
- 1982 People's Union for Democratic Rights v UOI(Asiad Workers Case) - payment below statutory minimum wage amounts to forced labor - forced term expands to economic compulsion & beyond physical coercion
- 1984 Neerja Chaudhary v State of MP - SC held that mere release of bonded laborers not sufficient, state must ensure effective rehabilitation

| Reasons                                                                                   | Measures to overcome                                                                                             |
| ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Chronic poverty, economic vulnerability                                                   | Strengthening legal enforcement -  DMs must proactively identify cases, not wait for complaints                  |
| Lack of access to formal credit                                                           | Expanded access to formal credit - Jan Dhan-Aadhar-Mobile (JAM)  - financial inclusion                           |
| Large informal economy - absent labor rights & social security                            | Formalizing the economy -  registration of workers - *e Shram portal* - ensuring portability for migrant workers |
| social exclusion & caste discrimination - 80% belong to vulnerable groups                 | Ensure effective rehab beyond financial compensation - skill training, education, housing - MGNREGA              |
| unregulated informal contract - advance wage deduction, inflated interests & debt figures | Regular inspections in high-risk sectors - brick kilns, mining, agriculture, construction -fast track courts     |
| Low awareness about legal protections                                                     | Land reforms, access to education, social awareness programs address structural inequalities                     |
### Innovative models
- Tamil Nadu's Proactive District model - surprise inspections in high-risk areas - brick kilns, rice mills - high rescue rate
- Telangana's Convergence Approach - linked rescued laborers with housing schemes, pension schemes & skill development programmers - comprehensive rehab
- Brazil's mobile inspection unit - surprise inspection in remote areas - rescues in agriculture & mining areas
- UK's modern slavery framework - Modern Slavery Act mandates transparency in supply chains- large companies must disclose measures taken against forced labor

### Conclusion
eradication requires shift from reactive to proactive model, followed by comprehensive rehab facilities. Legal enforcement must be combined with poverty alleviation, social justice, financial inclusion, corporate accountability & community awareness
