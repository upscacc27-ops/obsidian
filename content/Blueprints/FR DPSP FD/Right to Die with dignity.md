### Context
SC ordered AIIMS to form a Secondary Medical Board to evaluate passive euthanasia for an in vegetative state for 13 years

### Euthanasia & its types
- refers to intentional act of ending a person's life to relieve unbearable pain or suffering, especially in cases of terminal illness
- concept is rooted in idea of compassion & individual autonomy
- Types
	- Active Euthanasia - directly administering a lethal substance, intentionally ending a life, is illegal in most countries due to unethical concerns
	- Passive Euthanasia - withdrawing or withholding life-sustaining treatment, underlying illness causing natural death, legally permitted in several countries under strict safeguards

>[!Note] Living Will
>written legal document through which person states their wishes regarding treatment in case they become incapable of making decisions in future, allows individual to decide whether life-sustaining treatments such as ventilators etc. should be withheld. Aims at protecting **individual autonomy** & **dignified end of life**

### Legal Reports
- Law Commission of India, 2006 - distinguished between active & passive euthanasia, recommending legalization of passive under strict safeguards, emphasized need for medical boards to confirm terminal illness & patient consent
- Law Commission of India, 2012 - Reiterated support for passive euthanasia, recommended statutory recognition of advanced medical directives(legal wills) to honor autonomy

### Way forward
- Enacting clear legislations that define & govern life-support withdrawal in cases of terminal illness
- Training on ethical, legal & procedural aspects for medical staff
- streamlining the living will process- simplified & accessible process
- raising public awareness - informed choices

### Conclusion
- Euthanasia raise profound medical dilemmas, balancing autonomy, dignity & compassion against principle of non-maleficence(do no harm). 
- Should remain a carefully regulated option, reflecting moral responsibility & humane care
